Vaastu tips that actually matter — and the ones that don't

We've designed over 400 vaastu-compliant homes. Here's our honest take on which vaastu principles improve day-to-day living, and which are folklore you can safely set aside.

"Tradition that earns its place"

Why this essay exists

Every family that walks into our office asks about vaastu. Some want it strictly followed, some privately don't believe in it but worry about resale, some want our honest opinion. After 20 years, our honest opinion is this: vaastu at its best is ancient observational wisdom about sunlight, air, and human movement — dressed in spiritual language. When it aligns with those physical truths, it works. When it's just tradition for tradition's sake, it doesn't.

Here's how we sort one from the other.

The five vaastu principles that actually improve liveability

1. Main door facing North or East

Matters. Not because of spirits or prosperity — because north/east-facing entrances get gentler morning light, less harsh afternoon sun, and in Mangalore, dramatically better cross-ventilation during monsoons. Families with north/east main doors genuinely report more daylight in common areas.

2. Kitchen in the southeast (Agni corner)

Matters. Because the southeast gets morning sun (good for a kitchen) but is away from bedroom areas in most layouts. This placement naturally keeps cooking heat and smells from drifting into living/sleeping spaces.

3. Master bedroom in the southwest

Matters, partly. Southwest walls in Mangalore stay cooler later in the day — which helps in our hot April-May period. It's also the farthest point from the main door, providing natural privacy.

4. No toilets adjacent to kitchens or puja rooms

Matters. Plumbing issues aside, shared walls between kitchens and bathrooms create smell, moisture and noise transfer issues. This principle is basically good plumbing hygiene wrapped in tradition.

5. North-east corner kept open (no clutter)

Matters, behaviourally. We've observed that families whose living areas have an open, uncluttered corner catching morning light are more likely to sit there, read, do yoga. The "spiritual" framing encourages a habit that is objectively good for wellbeing.

The vaastu principles we don't stress over

Bathroom must face a specific direction

In modern apartments, bathroom orientation is dictated by plumbing stacks and common shafts — which are building-level structural decisions. Trying to "vaastu-correct" a single flat's bathroom rarely works and often reduces liveability.

No mirror opposite the bed

This one is aesthetic preference, not physics. Some people find it unsettling; others don't notice. Place mirrors wherever works for you.

Staircase curving clockwise

In apartments, staircases are building-common and can't be altered. Don't reject a good flat on this basis.

Specific colour for specific rooms

Vaastu colour recommendations are loose and often contradictory across different schools. Choose colours that feel right to you — muted, warm, and consistent with natural light.

Good design and good vaastu overlap about 80% of the time. Where they conflict, we side with good design — because you'll live with the consequences every day.

The Sindhu approach

Every Sindhu Corporation floor plan is designed with vaastu in mind from day one — not adjusted afterwards. That means:

  • Main entrances in north or east wherever site orientation permits
  • Kitchens placed in southeast or east
  • Puja rooms (where included) in the northeast
  • Master bedrooms in southwest
  • No adjacent kitchen/bathroom walls
  • Cross-ventilation and natural light prioritised above all else

What we don't do: add vaastu tick-boxes that make the actual living experience worse. Example: we won't shrink a living room to 9x9 just to get a "correct" northeast size. The result would look and feel worse. Compromises get made on vaastu rather than on liveability.

If vaastu matters deeply to you

Several of our past buyers have brought their own family vaastu consultants to review our floor plans before booking. We welcome this. In 95% of cases, they find the plans already align with major principles — because our in-house architects work with vaastu as one of several design inputs, not as an afterthought.

And for the 5% where something needs adjustment — interior rearrangement, entrance bell placement, puja room orientation — we work with the family to find sensible solutions without compromising the core architecture.

The bottom line

Vaastu isn't magic. But treated as a collection of observations about light, air, flow and focus, it holds up surprisingly well. Buy a home that feels right when you walk in — with good natural light, sensible layout, gentle movement patterns. That home will almost certainly be vaastu-compliant in the ways that matter.
